In AI-assisted workflows, the problem often does not appear as a single wrong answer. It appears as coordination friction: repeated handoffs, unclear checkpoints, rework, context loss, and fragile transitions between agents, tools, or human reviewers. SDVM starts by asking whether the workflow leaves enough operational evidence to make that degradation observable.

1. The workflow

A team uses an AI-assisted workflow to produce recurring technical review reports. Each run passes through drafting, tool-assisted evidence gathering, review, correction, and final decision.

2. The symptom

The final report may still look acceptable, but friction appears:

  • Same output quality, but more correction loops
  • Handoffs become less clear
  • Reviewer asks the same clarifying questions repeatedly
  • Tool outputs copied forward without context
  • Checkpoints happen late or inconsistently

3. Why output review is not enough

Final deliverable quality does not reveal coordination degradation. SDVM asks whether the process leaves evidence that makes friction observable across runs.

7. Controlled pilot (high level)

In a controlled pilot, the private SDVM engine would examine how coordination changes across comparable runs, where evidence suggests repeated friction, and whether interventions improve or degrade workflow coordination. Scoring and calibration details remain private.

8. Why this matters

Observable coordination degradation enables informed pilot decisions — not generic AI quality claims or blind deployment.
